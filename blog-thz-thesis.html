What a THz metamaterial thesis taught me

Research reflections • Sayek Shahriar

My undergraduate thesis was more than a technical requirement. It became one of the strongest experiences in shaping how I think, work, and solve problems as an engineer.

Working on a triple-band THz metamaterial sensor taught me that engineering research is not only about results. It is about learning how to stay patient while a problem slowly becomes clearer. At the beginning, the work felt highly theoretical. The terms, parameters, and simulations seemed abstract, and progress was slower than I expected. Over time, however, I began to understand how each design decision affected sensing behavior and why careful iteration matters so much in research.

One of the biggest lessons from the thesis was the value of structure. Research can become overwhelming very quickly when there are many variables, many possible directions, and no immediate final answer. I learned to break large questions into smaller ones. I began focusing on one change at a time, studying how each modification affected the sensing performance, and then using that information to guide the next step. That process gave me a stronger method for technical thinking beyond this specific project.

The tools I used also changed the way I approached engineering problems. CST helped me visualize system behavior in a more practical way, while OriginPro helped me analyze results and compare performance more carefully. Together, they taught me that software tools are not just technical requirements. They become extensions of how you think. They allow you to test assumptions, challenge your own ideas, and improve designs with evidence rather than guesswork.

Another important lesson was that theory becomes much more meaningful when it connects to real applications. My work on refractive index sensitivity was not simply an academic exercise. It opened up ways of thinking about practical use cases such as materials testing, diagnostics, and chemical analysis. That connection between theory and impact made the work far more motivating.

Most importantly, the thesis made me more confident in my ability to work through complex technical challenges. It showed me that even when progress is slow, consistency matters. Careful analysis matters. Curiosity matters. Research taught me discipline, and it also reminded me that engineering growth often happens quietly through repeated effort rather than instant success.
